Dominik Ritzenhoff First-Year Writing Professor Enos 1/31/19 Crispr, gene sequencing, and designer babies-- resistance against great potential You walk into the store slowly and look at the different base models. You choose one-- a blue teddy bear. Then you pick the stuffing. You walk over to the accessories section and throw on some clothes. You choose a suit because you like suits. They’re professional. Then you walk to the cashier and pay. You entered Build-A-Bear with an idea and left with a final product. Now imagine this for your child. You walk into the hospital and like Build-A-Bear, you choose the different attributes you want your child to have. Boy or Girl? Girl. Tall? Yeah, let’s make her 5’ 10’’. Oh, and I want her to have freckles and straight teeth. Braces are expensive. And like that, after a few hours, you walk out of the hospital carrying the embryo that in nine months will be your customized child. This is the kind of future that genetic engineering enables. For the first time ever, on the mass scale, parents will have the ability to customize their children. Whether it be height, good looks, or resistance to rare diseases, these children will have an edge over the rest of society. Although, it seems the more available gene editing becomes, the more it is met with resistance. The initial concept and resistance of designer babies were largely introduced after the publication of Brave New World in 1932. Throughout the book, the author, Aldous Huxley, describes a dystopian future in which each embryo is genetically programmed to fulfill a specific role in society. There are no more families, and there is little uniqueness about each child. It was then that Huxley ignited a fear of genetic modification, designer babies, and a future that could stem from these advancements into society. Today, this issue has come back. Only this time, the technology to make designer babies exists. CRISPR exists. Ok, what is CRISPR? Well, the acronym stands for Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeat. But what does that even mean? When a foreign virus invades a bacterium, it disperses its genetic material throughout it in order to reproduce, which will eventually kill the cell. In response, the bacterium sends out little enzyme fighters (think of them like pawns) to combat the virus. Very often, since the enzymes are not specialized to fight the specific virus, the bacterium dies; however, on the rare occasion that the bacterium does survive, little enzyme retrievers (like a dog retrieving a ball) pick up some pieces of the virus’s DNA and implant it into the bacterium’s DNA. The next time the same virus comes into the bacterium, the cell produces specialized assassin molecules that have mugshots of the virus’s DNA (from the previous encounter) and then go around and kill anything that matches the mugshot. Now, what does this process have to do with gene editing babies? Well, scientists discovered that they could give these assassin molecules a mugshot of a specific gene sequence that they wanted to remove. Once the assassins cut the DNA, splitting it into two, the scientists inject a new DNA sequence into the general vicinity of the break and then wait for the DNA enzyme maintainers to pick it up and implement it into the main DNA helix. Simply put, CRISPR lets us edit DNA like we edit an essay. This is the fundamental technology which has made Huxley’s dystopia possible. And with his dystopia comes his resistance. One argument against genetically engineering designer babies is that there is still so much unknown about the “side effects” of tampering with nature. What else happens to a baby if we make him one foot taller than he was originally programmed to be? We simply don’t know. In December of 2018, He Jiankui, a Chinese scientist, and his team at the Southern University of Science and Technology in Shenzhen admitted to taking such a risk-- they modified a human embryo with CRISPR: “they planned to eliminate a gene called CCR5 in hopes of rendering the offspring resistant to HIV, smallpox, and cholera”(Regalado). These three diseases are genetic and can be prevented by altering one part of the DNA. This seems ok-- after all, Jiankui was trying to prevent disease through genetic modification. Yet this caused an ethical dilemma which eventually led to his removal from the university. The implications of this scientist’s actions aren’t that he tried to prevent fatal genetic diseases. It’s that he opened a door which will be very difficult to close. With making this one change, it raises the questions of whether humanity can go further than just preventing diseases. Why not at that point customize your child to give him an edge over the rest of society? Also, the changes to that embryo will trickle down into the rest of society as that child’s descendants further diversify. There is no backspace option to the human genome. Changes are permanent. This scientist and his team were the first to make such changes to the human embryo. They had good intentions, but it doesn’t mean that every other team which uses CRISPR will. This is an ethical dilemma because it can change society as we know it. Designer babies could be the spark that ignites the flame for further genetic modification. Is this what we want? One of the defining aspects of evolution is that the world changes, which forces organisms to change to the new environment or die. Humans cannot directly control how the earth changes, but we can now control how an organism can adapt to the new environment. As more CO2 is being released into the atmosphere every year, it almost seems almost logical that we simply alter our babies to be able to live in such new environments. Society currently rejects the idea, but it could be instrumental to our survival as a species. It could come sooner than we think.
